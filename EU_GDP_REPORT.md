# EU GDP in 2025: Anomalous Growth, Sectoral Drivers, and Macro Trends

*Analysis date: May 19, 2026 — based on Eurostat provisional/final 2025 national accounts as of mid-2026*

---

## 1. Executive Summary

The EU-27 expanded at an unweighted-mean rate of **2.13% in real terms in 2025**. Stripping out Ireland (a 12.3% statistical outlier driven almost entirely by multinational pharmaceutical operations), the cross-country mean was **~1.7%** — a continuation of the post-2022 pattern of moderate growth, masking enormous divergence between member states.

Three structural fault-lines defined 2025:

1. **A US tariff shock**, announced and partially implemented under the Trump administration in early 2025, that simultaneously **inflated Ireland's headline GDP by triggering pharmaceutical export front-loading** [[2]][[3]][[8]] and **subtracted from Germany's net exports** in autos, machinery, and chemicals [[14]][[19]].
2. **A continuing German industrial recession** (manufacturing GVA -1.0%, construction -2.9%, finance -3.6%) in which the €500 billion Merz infrastructure/defence fund was legislated but barely deployed within calendar 2025 [[16]][[18]][[20]].
3. **A "convergence cluster" of Eastern/Southern outperformers** — Cyprus (3.8%), Poland (3.6%), Croatia (3.4%), Bulgaria (3.1%), Spain (2.8%), Denmark (2.9%), Malta (4.0%) — all benefiting from some combination of EU recovery funds, defence spending, tourism, euro convergence, or a specific concentrated industrial winner (Denmark's Novo Nordisk).

The most analytically important finding is that **Ireland's 12.3% is not a domestic boom**: Modified Domestic Demand grew only 4.9% and GNP only 2.4%, with €56.8 bn of additional factor income flowing back out to foreign multinationals [[1]]. The headline number is essentially measurement artefact of MNE production response to US tariff policy.

The most concerning finding is **Bulgaria's growth composition**: a 3.1% headline obscures an 8.0% collapse in manufacturing, with the entire expansion driven by an 11.9% nominal wage surge fuelling consumption, pre-euro-adoption confidence, and credit growth — financed by a -4.37 pp net-export drag [[8]][[9]][[10]].

## 2. Methodology & Data Notes

- **Real GDP growth, nominal GDP, HICP inflation, expenditure contributions, and sector GVA growth** all from Eurostat annual datasets (`tec00115`, `nama_10_gdp`, `prc_hicp_ainr`, `nama_10_a10`), accessed via the Eurostat dissemination API, updated 2026-04-23 and 2026-05-18 [[1]][[15]].
- **Current account and fiscal balance data** from IMF DataMapper (BCA_NGDPD, GGXCNL_NGDP).
- **Ireland-specific data** (MDD, GNP, Personal Consumption Expenditure) from CSO Ireland Quarterly National Accounts Q4 2025 release [[1]].
- **2025 figures provisional ("p")** for Belgium, Bulgaria, Croatia, Cyprus, France, Germany, Greece, Hungary, Luxembourg, Portugal, Romania, and Spain. Other countries' figures are unflagged but should still be considered subject to revision until at least late 2026.
- **Important caveat — Ireland**: Ireland's real GDP is heavily distorted by multinational IP relocation, contract manufacturing, and aircraft leasing flows. **GNI* or Modified Domestic Demand is the meaningful welfare measure**, not GDP. This report flags Ireland's figures as such throughout.
- **Sum check**: The expenditure-side pp contributions in Section 8 sum to within 0.01–0.02 pp of the headline GDP growth rate (Eurostat rounding artefact).

## 3. Regional Overview

The EU-27 aggregate context for 2025:

- **Monetary policy**: The ECB completed its rate-cutting cycle by Q3 2025, having reversed most of the 2022–2023 hikes. Eurozone inflation converged toward the 2% target through 2025, with HICP averages of 2.3% (Germany) to 0.9% (France) for the major economies. Romania (6.8%), Estonia (4.8%), Hungary (4.4%), Croatia (4.4%), and Slovakia (4.2%) remained inflation outliers.
- **Fiscal stance**: Aggregate EU fiscal deficits narrowed modestly but Romania (-8.0%), Poland (-6.2%), Belgium (-5.3%), France (-5.1%), and Slovakia (-4.9%) carried wide deficits. Denmark (+2.9%), Cyprus (+3.0%), Luxembourg (+1.2%), Ireland (+0.8%) ran surpluses.
- **External shock**: The Trump administration's 2025 tariff measures hit the EU's largest exporting economies asymmetrically. Germany absorbed the cost in auto, machinery and chemical exports [[14]][[19]]; Ireland and Denmark experienced front-loading benefits in pharmaceuticals [[2]][[3]][[6]]; smaller economies were largely insulated.
- **EU recovery funds**: NextGenerationEU/RRF disbursements were finally ramping after 2024 delays. Poland had committed ~45% of its envelope by mid-2025 [[24]]; Croatia, Spain, and Italy remained the heaviest absorbers. Romania's absorption was uneven and contributed to construction strength despite austerity elsewhere [[21]][[22]].

## 6. Anomaly Analysis — High Growth

### 6.1 Ireland (real GDP +12.3%): a measurement artefact, not a boom

**Sector signal**: Industry (B-E) GVA +29.1%; Manufacturing +31.0%; ICT (J) +11.5%; Construction +9.7%; Professional services (M-N) **-4.0%**; Other services (R-U) **-1.6%** [[15]].

**Expenditure signal**: GFCF contributed +6.32 pp; Inventories +4.40 pp; Exports +7.63 pp (imports -5.90 pp, net exports +1.73 pp); **Private consumption -0.32 pp** [[1]].

**The story** ([[2]][[3]][[4]][[5]][[6]][[8]]): Irish GDP in 2025 was overwhelmingly a US pharmaceutical front-loading and capacity expansion event. Reuters reported that "Ireland's Q1 GDP jump was due to a surge in pharma exports to the U.S. ahead of threatened tariffs" [[2]]. CSO trade data confirm: medical and pharmaceutical exports to the US reached €9.4 bn in January and €10.5 bn in February 2025, up from €1.9 bn in February 2024 [[3]]. For 2025 as a whole, pharma exports rose **+39.0% to €138.6 bn**, with US-bound exports rising +52.0% to €111.7 bn [[5]]. In March 2025 alone, pharma exports to the US reached €23.6 bn — a 243% year-on-year increase [[4]].

The Irish Fiscal Advisory Council (IFAC) examined whether this was purely inventory destocking (which would not boost GDP) or genuine new production. Their finding: while stocks fell €4.7 bn in Q1 2025 (the largest fall on record), this could not explain a Q1 pharma export increase of ~€33 bn. The remainder reflected **new Irish production capacity and new products, particularly protein- and peptide-based hormones used in GLP-1 weight-loss and diabetes medicines** — with US customs data confirming Indiana (Eli Lilly's home) as the primary destination [[8]].

**The domestic reality** ([[1]]): CSO Ireland reported Modified Domestic Demand growth of **4.9%**, GNP growth of **2.4%**, and Personal Consumption Expenditure growth of **2.9%** — all healthy but nothing like 12.3%. Factor-income outflows rose **42.2% (€56.8 bn)**, capturing the gap. Irish households did not get richer by 12%; foreign multinationals booked more profits in Ireland.

**Classification: STRUCTURAL artefact + CYCLICAL pharma surge**. The MNE-driven GDP/GNI* wedge is structural and permanent. The 2025 spike on top of it is a one-time tariff-front-loading event likely to reverse partially in 2026 — Bloomberg already reported Ireland fell into a technical recession in Q4 2025 as the front-loading exhausted itself [[7]].

### 6.2 Denmark (real GDP +2.9%): the Novo Nordisk economy meets the cycle

**Sector signal**: Manufacturing +9.2%; Industry (B-E) +8.3%; ICT +7.4%; **Construction -2.3%**; Finance -3.8%; Public services -1.6% [[15]].

**Expenditure signal**: Net exports +2.37 pp; Private consumption +1.00 pp; **GFCF -0.82 pp** [[1]].

**The story** ([[11]][[12]][[13]][[14]][[15-Den]]): Novo Nordisk remained the single largest macroeconomic force, but the *incremental* boost slowed sharply. Company revenues went DKK 232 bn (2023) → DKK 290 bn (2024) → DKK 309 bn (2025) — i.e., from ~+25% growth to ~+6% growth. Reuters described Novo as "losing ground to Eli Lilly" in November 2025, and the Danish central bank cut its growth forecast citing both US tariff risk and the pharma slowdown [[10D]][[11D]].

The negative GFCF contribution despite a manufacturing boom is informative. Novo's own capex *rose* from DKK 47.2 bn (2024) to DKK 60.1 bn (2025), but much of this spending was global (US capacity build-out) rather than Danish, and was offset by **weak national investment in housing, commercial real estate, offshore wind, and transport equipment** [[7D]]. Ørsted in particular was hit when the US ordered an offshore wind project halt in August 2025 [[16D]].

**Classification: STRUCTURAL (concentration risk)**. Denmark's GDP is now meaningfully dependent on a single firm's R&D pipeline. Sustainability depends on whether Novo can defend GLP-1 market share against Eli Lilly's Mounjaro/Zepbound and next-generation oral therapies.

### 6.3 Cyprus (+3.8%), Poland (+3.6%), Croatia (+3.4%): the convergence cluster

These three economies share the pattern: **broad-based growth led by services and investment, supported by EU funds and (for Cyprus and Croatia) tourism.**

- **Cyprus**: ICT (J) +8.0%, Trade/Transport/Hospitality (G-I) +6.4%, Construction +4.5%. Net exports contributed +2.45 pp [[1]][[15]]. The story is a continuing services boom — fintech, professional services to relocated Russian/Israeli/Ukrainian companies, and tourism. Fiscal surplus of +3.0% of GDP — one of the EU's tightest fiscal positions.

- **Poland** ([[23]][[24]][[26]][[27]]): Private consumption +2.11 pp; government consumption +1.09 pp; services M-N +5.2%; public services O-Q +4.4%. Three drivers:
 1. **Real wage growth** still positive despite easing — Bloomberg reported "Polish wage growth easing, strengthening rate-cut expectations" [[26]];
 2. **Defence spending at ~4.6-4.7% of GDP** — the highest in NATO — providing a fiscal-demand impulse and spillovers into procurement, logistics, and infrastructure [[27]];
 3. **RRF/NextGenerationEU funds finally flowing** — Poland had signed deals allocating ~45% of its envelope by mid-2025 after earlier rule-of-law delays [[24]].

- **Croatia** ([[28]][[29]][[30]][[31]]): Construction GVA +7.3% — the EU's strongest. GFCF contributed +1.55 pp. Drivers:
 1. **Tourism** — ~110 million tourist overnights in 2025, another near-record year [[28]];
 2. **Post-earthquake reconstruction** in Zagreb/Petrinja, with EU Solidarity Fund disbursements exceeding €1 bn [[29]];
 3. **NGEU/RRF absorption** sustaining public investment [[30]];
 4. **Euro adoption** (January 2023) lowering financing costs and supporting credit growth [[31]].

**Classification**: 
- Poland — **CYCLICAL with structural tailwinds**. Growth above EU average should persist but moderate from 3.6% toward 2.5–3.0% as wage impulse fades and RRF effect peaks.
- Croatia — **CYCLICAL**. Tourism, reconstruction, and NGEU all face natural end-dates. Overheating risks (services inflation, housing, current account) are real.
- Cyprus — **STRUCTURAL** (sustained services/financial centre transformation).

### 6.4 Bulgaria (+3.1%): the composition anomaly

**Sector signal**: Manufacturing GVA **-8.0%**; Industry (B-E) -3.4%; Construction +6.5%; Financial services +3.6%; Real estate +5.8% [[15]].

**Expenditure signal**: Private consumption **+4.48 pp** (the EU's largest); Government consumption +1.39 pp; GFCF +2.09 pp; **Net exports -4.37 pp** (the EU's worst) [[1]].

**The story** ([[32]][[33]][[34]][[35]][[36]][[37]][[38]]): Bulgaria was **cleared for euro adoption from January 1, 2026** during the course of 2025 (not 2025 as occasionally misreported) [[32]][[33]]. The IMF noted Bulgarian growth was "propelled by sustained private consumption, strong credit growth, and fiscal easing" with euro adoption prospects strengthening confidence [[34]]. The minimum monthly wage rose from BGN 933 to BGN 1,077 (+15.4%) on January 1, 2025 [[36]], and gross average wages were up **11.9% year-on-year** in September 2025 with total hourly labour costs up 12.3% in Q3 [[35]].

The manufacturing collapse is more diagnostic. Bulgarian industry was hit by **energy-intensive sector stress** (basic metals down 36% YoY in February, chemicals down 6.0%) [[37]], by **rising producer prices** (manufacturing PPI +10.8% YoY in November — the highest in the EU) [[37]], and by maintenance shutdowns at two major exporters [[38]]. Goods exports' share of GDP collapsed from a 2015–2022 average of 49% to an estimated 38% in 2025 [[37]].

National-accounts data show the result: final consumption +7.8% YoY, imports +11.1% YoY, exports +0.2% YoY [[38]].

**Classification: CYCLICAL (pre-euro convergence boom) on top of STRUCTURAL deterioration of industrial competitiveness**. The headline 3.1% looks healthy but masks an unsustainable internal-external mix. Watch for a "convergence hangover" in 2026–2027 once the consumption surge normalizes and the import bill remains elevated.

### 6.5 Malta (+4.0%)

**Sector signal**: Trade/Transport/Hospitality +8.8%; ICT +6.9%; Public services +5.6%; **Agriculture -23.2%** (statistical noise — small base); Manufacturing -0.9% [[15]].

**Expenditure signal**: Private consumption +1.50 pp; Government consumption +1.00 pp; Net exports +1.04 pp [[1]].

The Maltese economy continues to ride its services specialisation (online gaming, financial services, tourism). The deceleration from 10.6% (2023) and 6.2% (2024) to 4.0% (2025) reflects normalization rather than weakness. **Classification: STRUCTURAL** — the high-growth services-export model remains intact.

## 7. Anomaly Analysis — Low Growth

### 7.1 Germany (real GDP +0.2%): the structural industrial recession persists

**Sector signal**: Manufacturing -1.0%; Construction **-2.9%**; Financial services **-3.6%**; Professional services -0.6%; Public services +1.5% — the only meaningfully positive sector [[15]].

**Expenditure signal**: Net exports **-1.53 pp** (the EU's worst negative export contribution); Private consumption +0.82 pp; GFCF essentially flat at -0.04 pp; Inventories +0.70 pp [[1]].

**The story** ([[16]][[17]][[18]][[19]][[20]]): The Merz €500 bn infrastructure/defence fund was the major political event of 2025 — but the 2025 fiscal impulse was minimal. The 2025 budget was not approved until September, leaving little time for procurement and construction tenders to translate into activity. Reuters later reported that 2025 borrowing undershot plan because spending fell [[18]], and an IW Köln study found the fund had not yet spurred genuinely additional investment [[20]]. Bloomberg warned delivery bottlenecks risked the fund's effectiveness [[16]]. **The fiscal regime change was legislated, not yet deployed.**

The automotive complex remained in restructuring mode. VW pushed ahead with German cost cuts, paused production at certain EV plants [[19]], and Bosch announced major job cuts as the industry crisis deepened [[20]]. VDA data showed 2025 passenger-car production at roughly 4 million units — still well below pre-pandemic levels.

The Trump 2025 tariffs hit Germany asymmetrically because the export base is concentrated in exactly the targeted sectors: autos, machinery, chemicals. Reuters reported German auto exports were "hit hard" by the tariffs [[14]], translating into the -1.53 pp net export drag.

Chemicals continued the slow-motion relocation. VCI (the German chemicals lobby) lowered its 2025 outlook, and BASF continued cuts at Ludwigshafen [[19a]].

**Classification: STRUCTURAL recession with cyclical tariff overlay**. The 2026 outlook depends on (a) actual disbursement of the infrastructure fund, (b) whether US tariff policy is renegotiated, and (c) whether the auto industry stabilizes its EV transition.

### 7.2 Romania (+0.7%): austerity meets uncompetitive industry

**Sector signal**: Manufacturing **-3.4%**; Professional services (M-N) **-4.0%**; Construction **+6.9%**; Agriculture +6.6%; ICT +4.1% [[15]].

**Expenditure signal**: Government consumption **-0.48 pp (negative — i.e., real public consumption contraction)**; GFCF +0.87 pp; Private consumption +0.32 pp; Net exports -0.52 pp [[1]].

**The story** ([[21]][[22]]): Romania entered 2025 with the EU's widest fiscal deficit (~8% of GDP) and was under the Excessive Deficit Procedure. Two waves of consolidation followed:

1. **January 2025 (Ciolacu government emergency decree)**: dividend tax 8→10%, special construction tax reintroduced, IT/construction/agriculture sector tax exemptions removed, public wage freeze, microenterprise threshold cut.
2. **July 2025 (Bolojan government package)**: VAT 19→21%, excise hikes, dividend tax to rise further to 16% in 2026, bank/financial-sector taxation increased, public wage freeze extended into 2026.

The unique pattern — **construction +6.9% while government consumption negative** — reflects that Romania protected the EU-funded investment pipeline (RRF, cohesion funds) while squeezing current expenditure. Romania's RRF envelope is ~€28.5 bn though absorption has been uneven [[22]].

Manufacturing weakness reflected auto (Dacia/Renault Mioveni, Ford Otosan Craiova) production declines, energy-intensive industry stress, and broader competitiveness loss from prior wage growth and a stable leu producing real appreciation.

**Classification: CYCLICAL (austerity-driven) with STRUCTURAL competitiveness erosion**. Fiscal consolidation will continue to weigh in 2026, but the EU-funded investment channel should keep growth positive.

### 7.3 Italy (+0.5%), France (+0.8%), Austria (+0.6%): mature economies in slowdown

These large/mid economies all decelerated from 2024 levels. Italy's Manufacturing was flat to negative (-0.3%), reflecting the fade of the Superbonus construction stimulus (Construction +2.4% in 2025 vs much stronger in prior years). France's sector data show Manufacturing essentially flat (+0.4%) and Construction down -1.1%; the +6.9% Agriculture reading is a base effect from poor 2024 harvests. Austria — like Germany — was in industrial recession (Manufacturing +1.2% but Construction -2.9%, B-E -0.7% overall).

**Classification: CYCLICAL** for France and Italy (each has macro tailwinds — Italian RRF, French ECB rate relief — that should reaccelerate growth modestly in 2026). Austria is more **STRUCTURAL** given its tight integration with the German industrial complex.

### 7.4 Finland (+0.2%), Hungary (+0.5%), Slovakia (+0.8%)

These three Central/Northern economies share a sluggish industrial cycle. Hungary in particular shows broad weakness: Agriculture -4.3%, Manufacturing -2.6%, Real estate -1.5%, all consistent with the impact of tight monetary policy combined with weak EU industrial demand spilling into the German-Hungarian auto supply chain.

Finland's Manufacturing actually grew +1.9%, but this was offset by weakness in Construction (-1.5%), Trade (-1.4%), Finance (-4.6%), Public services (-1.1%), and Arts/other services (-3.3%) — a notably broad-based malaise. Finland has structural challenges from the loss of Russian trade after 2022.

## 10. Macroeconomic Themes & Root Causes

Four cross-cutting forces explain the 2025 EU pattern:

**Theme 1: The Trump tariff shock created winners and losers within the same continent.**
The same policy event — US tariffs introduced in early 2025 — boosted Ireland's headline GDP by ~10 percentage points via pharmaceutical front-loading [[2]][[3]][[5]][[8]] while subtracting from Germany's net exports [[14]] and clouding Denmark's outlook [[11D]]. Smaller economies (Croatia, Bulgaria, Poland) were largely insulated because they export less directly to the US.

**Theme 2: The Eastern/Southern convergence engine is intact.**
Six economies (Malta, Cyprus, Poland, Croatia, Bulgaria, Spain) all grew 2.8–4.0% — well above the EU mean. The common ingredients: EU funds (NGEU/RRF/cohesion), competitive labour costs, real wage convergence supporting consumption, and (for the Mediterranean) tourism. Where these converge with single-firm or single-sector concentration (Cyprus fintech, Maltese gaming, Croatian tourism), growth is high but vulnerability is real.

**Theme 3: The German-anchored industrial bloc is in structural recession.**
Germany (+0.2%), Austria (+0.6%), Hungary (+0.5%), Finland (+0.2%), and Slovakia (+0.8%) all sit in the bottom quintile. This is not coincidence — they are linked by the German automotive and machinery supply chain, by integration with German chemicals, and (in Finland's case) by adjacent loss of Russian demand. The Merz €500 bn fund could break this dynamic if deployed effectively in 2026–2027, but it failed to do so in 2025 [[16]][[18]][[20]].

**Theme 4: Fiscal divergence is widening.**
Romania (-8.0%), Poland (-6.2%), France (-5.1%), Belgium (-5.3%), and Slovakia (-4.9%) carried deficits that exceeded the 3% Maastricht threshold by wide margins. Romania began austerity in 2025; France and Belgium have not yet meaningfully consolidated. Meanwhile, Denmark (+2.9%), Cyprus (+3.0%), Ireland (+0.8%), and Luxembourg (+1.2%) ran surpluses. This divergence is creating two-speed fiscal politics within the eurozone.

## 11. Policy Context

**Monetary policy**: The ECB's 2025 rate-cutting cycle helped rate-sensitive economies (housing in the Netherlands, Spain, Sweden) but came too late to rescue Germany. Variable-rate economies (Finland, Sweden, Denmark) saw faster transmission than fixed-rate France.

**Fiscal policy**: 2025 was a transition year — France delayed consolidation through political instability; Romania front-loaded it; Germany legislated stimulus but failed to deploy it in time; Poland leaned on defence spending [[27]]; Bulgaria loosened fiscal policy ahead of euro entry [[34]].

**EU-level recovery funds**: NGEU/RRF disbursements finally accelerated in 2025 after the 2023–2024 delays. Poland (~45% committed by mid-2025 [[24]]), Croatia (>€1 bn from EU Solidarity Fund alone [[29]]), Italy, and Spain were the largest absorbers. Romania struggled with absorption but kept the infrastructure pipeline open as a deliberate policy choice [[22]].

**Trade policy**: US tariffs are the dominant external policy event. The asymmetric impact within the EU has not yet provoked a unified EU response, but it is reshaping investment decisions — particularly in chemicals and autos.

**Energy policy**: Industrial electricity prices in Germany remained structurally above US/China levels despite the 2022 crisis passing. The relocation of energy-intensive production continued at a slower pace through 2025 [[19a]].

## 12. Risks & Forward-Looking Assessment

**Short-term (2026):**

1. **Ireland reversal**: Bloomberg already reported Ireland fell into a Q4 2025 technical recession as pharma front-loading exhausted itself [[7]]. Expect Irish headline GDP growth to fall sharply in 2026 — possibly to negative — while MDD remains positive. Markets should focus on MDD as the genuine signal.

2. **Bulgaria post-euro-adoption inflation jump**: Lagarde explicitly warned of a temporary inflation jump from euro changeover [[33a]]. Combined with the already-strong wage and consumption cycle, Bulgaria faces meaningful overheating risk in 2026.

3. **German recovery contingent on fund deployment**: The 2026 fiscal impulse from the €500 bn fund will be the dominant variable for German growth. If procurement bottlenecks persist [[16]], a fourth consecutive year of near-stagnation becomes likely.

4. **Tariff renegotiation risk**: A US-EU trade deal would help Germany; further escalation would hurt Germany and Denmark and accelerate Irish reversal.

**Medium-term (2027+):**

5. **Croatian and Polish growth deceleration is likely** as NGEU funds taper and Croatia's reconstruction completes. Both should remain above the EU average but moderate from current levels.

6. **The German industrial complex needs a structural answer**, not just stimulus. The combination of high energy costs, EV transition costs, Chinese competition, and US tariffs is a structural challenge that cyclical policy cannot solve.

7. **Denmark concentration risk**: A negative pipeline event at Novo Nordisk would have outsized macro consequences. The economy needs a second growth engine.

8. **Romanian fiscal path**: The Bolojan consolidation should narrow the deficit toward the 5–6% range in 2026, but the political backlash means execution risk is high.
